Chapter One
Arrival
It isn't a door. I need to stop thinking of it that way. A door implies an opening, a handle you can reach for. What I've been measuring is closer to a wound. A place where the tissue between two systems has worn thin enough to tear.

I still have not shared my findings with anyone. (or anything).
— Henry Kairos, Personal Research Notebook, Entry 64

Theo woke to the taste of copper and the knowledge that he was not where he had been.

Not groggy. Not slow. The opposite. Every nerve firing at once, the way the body responds to a car wreck before the mind catches up.

He was on his back. Something soft and warm beneath him, giving slightly under his weight like packed moss, except it wasn't moss. It pulsed. Faintly. A rhythm he could feel in his spine.

He opened his eyes and the sky was wrong.

Two suns. One low and swollen, the other high and sharp, both casting light that skewed blue in a way that made every color beneath them feel shifted, like a monitor with the white balance pulled too cold. The canopy above him was dense. Amber-gold. Not leaves as he understood leaves but flat, translucent panels of something biological, layered and overlapping, filtering the dual light into a haze that settled on everything like pollen.

Theo sat up. His hands pressed into the ground and the ground pressed back. Firm, then yielding, then firm again. Adaptive. Learning his weight distribution.

He pulled his hands away fast.

"Faye."

She was six feet to his left, on her side, one arm folded under her head. Not moving. His chest seized for a half-second before he saw the rise of her ribs.

"Faye." Louder.

She inhaled sharp and rolled onto her back. Her eyes opened and she stared straight up at the canopy and didn't blink for a long time.

"Something's wrong with the light," she said.

"Yeah."

She sat up. Touched the ground with both palms. Drew them back the same way he had. Looked at him. Her expression wasn't fear yet. It was the thing right before fear, the part where the brain is still running calculations, still trying to match what the senses are reporting against every known category. He recognized it because his was doing the same thing.

"My phone is gone," she said. She patted her jacket, her pockets, the ground around her.

"Mine too." He checked his pockets methodically. Left front, right front, back left, back right, jacket interior. All empty except for the clothes on his back and his bag that contained his dad's notebook and the stone.

Henry's stone. It was in the interior pocket of his jacket, the one closest to his chest, and it was warm. Not the residual warmth of body heat. Warm like it had its own source. He left it where it was.

Faye was standing now, turning in a slow circle. Her hair was messy on one side, pressed flat from the ground. She was cataloging. He could see it in how her eyes moved: systematic, left to right, pausing at intervals.

"The trunks," she said.

He looked. The trees, if they were trees, had trunks the width of small cars, dark with what looked like ridged texture. Running through the bark in branching patterns were veins of light. Green-gold, dim, steady. Bioluminescence. He'd seen photos of glowing fungi deep in the ocean, but this was different. The light wasn't decorative or incidental. It looked structural. Vascular.

"They look like they're glowing," he said.

"Bioluminescent. Some kind of reaction, but at this scale..." She trailed off. Walked toward the nearest trunk and stopped three feet away. The veins in the bark shifted. Not much. A slight intensification. The green-gold warming toward blue where she stood closest.

She stepped back. The blue faded. She stepped forward. It returned.

"It's responding to me." She said it slowly, like she was hearing herself say it. Her voice had flattened the way it did when she was processing something that scared her.

"To us," Theo said. He'd been watching the trunks behind her. Two of them had done the same thing when he stood up. He'd written it off as his eyes adjusting. It wasn't.

"To us," she said while looking around.

Neither of them moved for a moment. The forest hummed around them. Not literally. But there was a rhythm to it, a slow oscillation in the ambient light that felt like respiration, and it was impossible not to map it onto something alive.

"Where are we?" Faye asked.

"I don't know." He was looking around while he said it. Not searching. Scanning.

She looked at him and he saw the fear now. Right behind her eyes, held in place by will. Faye Chen didn't panic. She went faster. Thought harder. Measured more.

But there was nothing here to measure. Her instruments were gone, and the forest was watching her back.

"Okay," Theo said as he thought. "Here's what we know." He held up fingers. "We're not where we were. The device brought us somewhere. The air is breathable. Gravity is close to Earth-normal but not exact."

"How do you know that?"

"Jump." He was already in the air when he said it.

She jumped. Not high, just a small hop. She stayed airborne a fraction of a second too long. Came down a fraction of a second too late.

"Possibly lower surface gravity," she said.

"The air tastes wrong too. You notice?"

She inhaled through her mouth, held it, exhaled slowly through her nose. "Mineral. Something metallic. And something I can't place. Like..." She searched for it. "Like the smell of a used bookstore but as a taste."

That was close to what he'd been thinking. Old paper. Old dust. Something preserved.

"Two suns," she continued, looking up. "Both visible simultaneously. We're probably orbiting the larger one with the smaller as a companion, or we're in a circumbinary orbit." She was talking faster now. "The canopy's filtering most of the UV. We'll have to test whether direct sunlight is safe."

"Faye." Sharp. Quiet.

"What?" Loud whisper.

"We need to move."

She stopped. Looked at him. "Move where?"

"High ground. If there's any. We need to see the landscape. Find water. Figure out if there's anyone else here, and if there is, whether they're a threat."

"We should stay put. Standard survival protocol. Stay where you are. Let rescue come to you." She said it like she was reading from a manual she'd memorized but never expected to use.

"Nobody knows we're here, Faye. Nobody's coming."

That landed. She flinched, a small one, but he saw it. He hadn't meant it to be cruel. It was the assessment. They'd driven to a research site at San Gorgonio Pass at night. No one had known they were there.

"High ground," Faye said after a moment. "Fine. Which direction?"

Theo looked around. The forest was dense in every direction. No obvious elevation change. The dual light from above was diffuse, scattered by the canopy into a uniform amber glow that cast no hard shadows and gave no directional cues.

He crouched and pressed his face to the ground. "This way slopes down," he said, pointing left. "Which means uphill is this way." He pointed right and started walking.

After a moment, Faye followed.

···

This forest didn't want them to be comfortable. That was the only way Theo could frame it. Not that the forest was hostile. Hostile implied intent, and he wasn't ready to assign intent to vegetation. But every detail conspired to keep his nervous system at full alert.

The light tracked them. Every trunk they passed responded. The green-gold veins brightening, shifting blue, holding the shift for exactly as long as they were in proximity, then fading back. A wavefront of attention following their path through the trees. Like walking through a room where every eye turned to follow you, except the eyes were in the walls.

The sounds were wrong. Not silent. Populated with low tones, resonant hums at the edge of hearing, clicks that came in irregular clusters. Biological sounds, probably. But nothing he could map to an animal or an insect or a bird. No categories. Just noise from living things he couldn't see, in a frequency range that sat right in the zone where the human brain starts assigning threat.

The colors kept catching him. He kept thinking his eyes would adjust, but they didn't, because this wasn't a matter of adjustment. The spectrum here was different. Whatever the two suns produced, filtered through whatever atmosphere this was and whatever biological pigments dominated the canopy, the result was a palette with no equivalent in his experience. The amber wasn't amber. It was something between amber and a color that didn't exist on Earth. The blue of the reactive bioluminescence wasn't blue. It was adjacent to blue. Close enough to file under "blue" but wrong enough to trigger a persistent, low-grade unease.

···

Forty minutes. Maybe an hour. Hard to tell without a clock, without the position of a single identifiable sun, without any frame of reference at all. Theo had been counting his steps. Around 4,000. Average stride length at this gravity, accounting for the slightly longer airborne phase, maybe 3 feet. Maybe two and a half miles.

The canopy thinned. Not gradually. The transition was abrupt, like a tree line, except it wasn't altitude-driven. One step they were in the dense, amber-lit interior. The next, the trunks were spaced twenty feet apart, the canopy open enough to show the sky.

Theo stopped. Faye stopped beside him.

The sky.

Four moons. He counted them twice. Four. Different sizes, different apparent phases. One was full and enormous, hanging low on what he arbitrarily designated the western horizon. It was the color of bone. The second was smaller, higher, half-lit. The third was a crescent, barely visible. The fourth was dark, visible only as an absence against the stars.

The stars. Dense. Far denser than anything he'd seen from Highland, from San Gorgonio Pass, from anywhere on Earth. Not the smear of the Milky Way. Individual points, thousands of them, in configurations that bore no resemblance to any constellation he knew.

Faye sat down. She sat down right where she was standing, on the warm yielding ground, and put her face in her hands.

He let her. He stood and looked at the sky and let her have the thirty seconds it took for the full weight to arrive.

They were not on Earth. They were not in any place reachable by any technology that they knew existed. They were wherever Henry's device had sent them, and they had no map, no communication, no rescue, and no idea how to get back.

"Okay," Faye said. She took her hands away from her face. Her eyes were dry. "I'm done."

"Done?"

"With that. The part where you want to collapse. I'm done with it. What's next?"

Theo thought for a moment. "Water. We need water."

"I haven't seen any." Her voice had flattened again.

"I haven't either. But the ground is wet. There's moisture in this system. Follow it downhill and you'll find collection. Follow it uphill and you'll find the source."

"You said we were going uphill." Half question, half accusation.

"We were. I want to see what's ahead before we commit to a direction." Theo said, looking around.

The thinned canopy gave them a view. The terrain ahead rose into a long, low ridge. Not steep. A few hundred feet of elevation gain over what looked like half a mile. The ridge was bare of the large trunks, covered instead in something low and dense, like ground cover, glowing faintly in the dim light. Beyond the ridge, the sky.

And on the ridge, silhouetted against the bone-colored moon, structures.

Theo saw them and felt his stomach drop. Not rock formations. Not trees. Structures. They had geometry. Rising from the ridge in shapes that suggested habitation, or at least purpose. Tall, tapered, with openings that could be windows or vents or something he had no word for. They caught the moonlight and the bioluminescent ground cover and seemed to glow from within. Like the trunks. Like everything here.

"Faye." Barely a whisper.

"I see them," she whispered. Theo's eyes widened. "Those are buildings."

"Or growths. Or geological formations." She was scanning the horizon. "We can't assume."

"They have windows." He pointed.

"Openings. We can't assume." She responded like she was defending a thesis.

"Faye. They have windows." Theo repeated more bluntly.

She didn't argue again.
